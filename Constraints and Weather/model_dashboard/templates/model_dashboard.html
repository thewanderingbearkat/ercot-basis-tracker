From "what is basis" to scenario analysis — the whole picture

1

What is "basis," and why do we care?

Electricity doesn't have one price — it costs different amounts at different spots on the grid. Basis is the price right at our wind farm's connection point minus the regional hub price. When basis is negative, our power is worth less than the hub — we're getting paid below the "headline" market price.

For a wind project, basis is real money. If the hub is $30 and our node settles at $5, that −$25 basis comes straight off every MWh we generate. Predicting when basis collapses is predicting a chunk of project revenue.
2

The one mechanism behind it here: the West Texas export constraint

West Texas builds far more wind and solar than the transmission lines heading east can carry. When the region generates more than the wires can export, the grid operator has to "back down" local prices to relieve the overloaded path — the West→North export GTC (a transmission limit) binds. Local prices crash; basis blows out. This happens most on high-wind nights (lots of wind, little local demand) and high-sun afternoons (solar flood) — a renewable over-supply regime.

3

How the model is built

We took 3 years of history at hourly resolution: for every hour, what the grid conditions were (wind, solar, load, outages, time of year) and what basis actually turned out to be — about 21,000 examples. A machine-learning model (gradient-boosted trees) reads all of that and learns the pattern: which combinations of conditions tend to crush basis. Nobody hand-sets the weights — the model derives them, and it independently landed on the export-constraint story above.

4

What feeds into it

The inputs split into three kinds — this distinction is what makes it a forecasting tool, not just a backward-looking fit:

Nature: West wind (Xweather) Nature: solar GHI (Xweather) Lever: ERCOT & West load Lever: transmission outages Calendar: hour / season / weekday

Nature inputs come from a real Xweather forecast at the Bearkat node — that's what lets us run forward. Levers are things we can dial in a what-if. Calendar sets the context.

5

The statistical engine — a range, not a single guess

Hourly basis is spiky and partly random, so a single predicted number would be false precision. Instead the model outputs a distribution for each hour:

q10 / q50 / q90 — the low, typical, and high cases. 8 times out of 10, basis lands between q10 and q90. Plus a blowout probability: the model's odds that basis drops below −$20 that hour. It's a risk gauge, not a crystal ball — tested forward, it ranks blowout-prone hours correctly about 82% of the time (50% would be a coin flip).

That's the honest framing for an M&A case: we can't promise the exact basis on a Tuesday, but we can say how likely and how severe a blowout is under given conditions.

6

The live demo — how that becomes the tracker

Each day we feed the next 7 days of Xweather forecast into the trained model and log the resulting band + blowout odds. As the hours arrive, we compare them to realized basis. That comparison is the Live tracker tab — and every scored hour accumulates into the track record, so over time we learn exactly which grid conditions the model reads well and where it's fooled.

7

Scenario analysis — the structural levers (the future)

History tells us how the grid behaved as it was. But an M&A case asks about things that haven't happened yet: a new data center lands behind the constraint, a new line gets built, load grows past any historical peak. The statistics can't see those — so the tool switches to physics (transmission shift factors: Δflow = sensitivity × ΔMW). Here's how each scenario variable routes:

Variable you changeHandled byWhy
Load scaled up/down (within history)statisticalThe model has seen these levels — just sample them.
Weather / wind / solar conditionsstatisticalSampled from the historical envelope via the Xweather forecast.
A NEW large load behind the nodestructuralNever happened — compute the added flow on the constraint directly.
New generation added at the nodestructuralChanges export volume; physics, not history.
New transmission / line upgradestructuralChanges the grid's wiring — needs recomputed shift factors.
The hand-off is the whole design. The statistical backbone gives a calibrated baseline inside known conditions; when a scenario steps outside that envelope, an out-of-sample detector flags it and the structural model takes over. Two tools, one number — "physics is stationary even when statistics aren't."

3 · Statistical engine — scikit-learn

gradient-boosted trees on the forecastable features

  • HistGradientBoostingRegressor (quantile q10 / q50 / q90) → the forecast band
  • HistGradientBoostingRegressor (mean) → the expected value used for budgeting
  • HistGradientBoostingClassifier → blowout probability (basis < −$20/MWh)
  • 12-month recency weighting so the fit tracks the current (worsening) regime, not the flat 3-year average
  • IsolationForest + per-feature support → the out-of-sample detector
↓
4 · Structural decisions (the assumptions)
  • Statistical ↔ structural hand-off: conditions inside history → trust the statistics; outside it (a brand-new load, demand past any historic peak) → hand off to physics.
  • GTC physics (structural.py): NBOHR basis = −(shadow price × shift factor), where the shift factor is a near-constant of the grid's wiring. New-load lever ≈ +$1.1/GW.
  • ATC vs GWA: weight basis by McCrae's actual generation → the wind-revenue number (what the project earns), not the all-hours average.
  • Per-driver sensitivities (sensitivities.py) feed the scenario calculator.
