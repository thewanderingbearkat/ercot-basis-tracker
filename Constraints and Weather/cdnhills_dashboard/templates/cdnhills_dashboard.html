What moves congestion from here

Direction & rough magnitude of the forward drivers in nFront’s assumptions. The renewable build-out dwarfs the load growth, and the transmission relief is back-loaded to the 2030s — so the near-term push on Canadian Hills basis is negative.

nFront’s forecast — and the risks vs. our data

What nFront is saying: this deliverable is an assumptions / transmission-assessment study — it does not state an explicit forward basis number for Canadian Hills (that would be a separate results/budget file, as with McCrae). Its forecast logic: run SPP SCED dispatch under +~12 GW new wind / +20 GW solar, ~2–3%/yr load growth, and transmission upgrades phasing in 2032–2037. Implied path: congestion stays heavy near-term, relieved only as those upgrades land. They proxy with Kingfisher and use the SPP South hub (both confirmed against our data).

Risks against the data we have:

  • Timing slip (high): SPP ITP (2032–37), Grain Belt HVDC (PJM Phase 2 withdrew Nov’25), MISO Tranche 1 are chronically delayed — if they slip, the bad-basis window extends.
  • Build-out outpaces relief: the new VER hits the same constrained paths before the upgrades; our realized basis is already −$7 to −$27, and more supply pushes it the wrong way.
  • Proxy & curtailment: Kingfisher is adjacent, not identical; and nFront’s curtailment context is SPP-wide (~few %) while Canadian Hills’ own is ~60% — a basis-only read understates the hit.
  • Recent calm looks fragile: realized basis eased to −$4 to −$7 in 2025–26 (from −$22 in 2024), but that’s before the build-out wave, so it likely doesn’t hold.

How it differs from McCrae

Canadian Hills' economics are dominated by lost volume, not the price on what it sells. In 2024–25 roughly 60% of generation was curtailed before it could reach the market — on the order of — of energy at hub value, every year.

Contrast with McCrae (ERCOT) → McCrae is barely curtailed (~3%); its damage is price suppression — it sells the volume but earns a poor generation-weighted basis (~−$3 to −$5). Canadian Hills earns a similar delivered basis only because it's switched off in the bad hours; its real hit is the 60% it never gets to sell. See the McCrae congestion model →

Same underlying mechanism: SPP wind over-supply binds the export path, so windy hours crater the price (calm-hour basis −$6 vs windy-hour −$22) — and SPP responds by curtailing. For an M&A case the key sensitivities are curtailment trajectory (does transmission relief arrive?) and the basis on delivered energy.
